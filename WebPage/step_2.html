Step 2. Define service offerings


Once your target polygons and priority targets have been established, it's time to define your Network's service offering. This consists of the services per se, but also the sales and marketing strategy. Information collected during step 1 will also inform and support the decisions that need to be made in this second step.



2.1 Describe your services

First, define which services will be offered and whether the service offering will be based on client segments such as residential, business, institutions. In addition, you should analyze if Public / Community Wi-Fi will be part of the service offering.

For each service, define the following characteristics:

  • Type of client — Is this a residential or a business service? Does it address a particular need from a type of institution such as hospitals or schools? Is it Public Wi-Fi? The separation of services based on the type of client will depend on the connectivity needs that you have identified during step 1 and the number of potential clients by type.
    It's important to take into account that if you provide services for distance learning and telemedicine, you may apply for a grant —see section 3.3 —.
  • Downlink and uplink speed — An important definition and a way to differentiate services is based on speed. As established by the FCC, the minimum service requirement is 25 Mbit/s on the downlink and 3 Mbit/s on the uplink — or 25/3 —; thus, you should have at least one service compliant to these speeds or higher.
    During step 1, you should discover if the potential subscribers of the Network will be heavy or light users of the Network based on their connectivity needs — gaming and 4k video streaming are applications that call for higher speeds —. For more guidance on required speeds for specific applications, go here.
    Furthermore, you may also discover if subscribers are willing to pay more for higher speeds or looking for the basic broadband service only.
  • Pricing — Establish the price and billing scheme for your service. The price of the service must specify the following:
    • Upfront fee to cover cost of customer installation and equipment. A level of subsidy of this cost can also be defined
    • Monthly rate
    • Billing scheme, i.e., if the service will be pre-paid or post-paid.
  • Customer relationship management (CRM) — Subscribers — actual and potential — will need to contact you for sales and support regarding technical, billing, and other issues. Thus, you must establish the CRM level, which means the channels and availability to provide the mentioned support. The most common service level is 8 hours a day, 5 days a week — 8x5 —; however, you may consider a higher service level such as 16x5, 8x7 or 24x7.

Once you have established these attributes for your service, you can write them down using the Business Plan template. This is an important task since the service descriptions will be used to engage with vendors during step 6, and to fill the business plan in step 7.



2.2 Define a sales strategy

The sales strategy considers how your clients can contract and pay for the services offered by the Network.

Common options to contract the service are:

  • At a tribal community building or central office
  • By phone
  • Through the Tribal Wireless Internet Service Provider (WISP) webpage
  • At local businesses that act as distributors

    • Regarding payment methods, you can consider the following:

  • On-site payment at a community building or central office
  • Through local businesses that act as distributors
  • Electronic payment
  • Bank deposit/check

The choice of contracting and payment options will depend on several factors such as client preferences — based on information gathered in step 1 —, tribal leadership guidelines, size of your tribal land, community infrastructure, among others. You must consider these factors and agree on the strategy with key stakeholders.

When your sales strategy is ready, write it down using the Business Plan template. This will facilitate the business plan preparation in step 7 and may also help during discussions with your tribal leadership.



2.3 Define a marketing strategy

The marketing strategy consists of the actions and programs that will be driven to promote service adoption in the target areas. The critical aspects of evaluating and defining the marketing strategy are: reach — how many customers can be reached —, impact — how effective is the marketing convincing customers to buy the service —, and cost.

When your marketing strategy is ready, write it down using the Business Plan template. This will facilitate the business plan preparation in step 7 and may also help during discussions with your tribal leadership.



2.4 Forecast subscribers

To forecast subscribers, you must first estimate the "adoption rate" for each service, which is the percentage of households and/or businesses that are expected to contract the service. For example, if there are 500 households in your target polygons, a 10% adoption rate would mean that 50 households will likely sign up for the service.

The estimation of the adoption rate is used during the design process to dimension the Network and during the financial analysis to calculate the revenue obtained from network services.

For each service, you should establish the "long-term" adoption rate. This means the adoption rate after the first few years of network operation, usually 5 years. This 5-year adoption rate can be estimated based on the percentage of people that would like to sign-up for the service during your data collection in step 1.

After this task, step 2 is completed; now you can continue to step 3 or issue an RFI to start analyzing vendor solutions to deploy your Network, as indicated in step 6.
